The Unlikely Encounter

A quiet afternoon where two strangers meet without knowing what it will become.

Personal Blog • March 2, 2026

The Unlikely Encounter

Estimated reading time: --

Dhaka in February belonged to books. Every year, the Ekushey Boi Mela transformed the Bangla Academy grounds into something almost sacred — a city within a city, where the smell of fresh ink hung in the cool air like incense, and strangers became friends over dog-eared pages and heated literary debates. Colorful banners rippled above the stalls, the distant microphone from the cultural stage carried fragments of poetry into the crowd, and everywhere one looked, there were books — towering, gleaming, waiting.

It was here, on an afternoon that seemed no different from any other, that two people walked unknowingly toward each other — and toward something neither of them was prepared for.

Nafis had seen the Instagram story that morning — Antik Mahmud, posted casually the way he posted everything, a short clip of himself at a stall surrounded by copies of his new book and the latest comic, announcing he'd be at the Mela that afternoon. Nafis had watched it twice, put his phone down, and started getting ready. He had been a famtik since class nine — since the year he had stumbled onto one of Antik's early animated videos late one night, watched it, then watched twelve more, then stayed up until two in the morning going through the entire channel. The animation had been rough at the edges back then, but the storytelling had been something else entirely — honest and funny and completely its own thing. He had followed everything since: the YouTube channel as it grew, the comics, the books when those came later.

Missing Antik Mahmud at the Boi Mela was simply not something he intended to do.

He arrived to find the stall without much difficulty — it wasn't hard to spot, a modest crowd of maybe fifteen or twenty people gathered around it, the kind of gathering that forms naturally when word spreads through the right channels rather than the wrong ones. Not a mob, just famtiks — recognizable by the way they held their copies, the way they were already talking to each other with the comfortable ease of people who had been in the same online spaces for years without ever meeting in person.

Nafis joined the loose edge of the group, craning slightly to see past a few people in front of him. Antik was at the center of it — signing a copy, saying something that made the fan in front of him laugh — looking, as he always did in his videos, entirely comfortable in the middle of things.

Nafis shifted to find a better angle and in doing so stepped squarely onto the foot of the person beside him.

There was a sharp intake of breath. He turned.

A girl his age was looking at him with the controlled expression of someone absorbing mild pain while deciding not to make it anyone else's problem. She was holding two items — a copy of the new book and what looked like an older comic volume, its spine visibly worn — and had apparently been standing there long enough to feel entitled to her square foot of ground, which, in fairness, she was.

"Sorry," he said.

"It's fine," she said, in the tone that means it is mostly fine but also that he should perhaps be more careful.

He moved slightly to the left to give her space. They both turned back toward the front. A minute passed.

"Is that the Noman series?" he asked, nodding at the comic under her arm. He hadn't been able to help noticing — the cover was familiar.

She glanced at him sideways. "Second volume. I brought it to get signed." A pause. "I've had it since it came out."

"The second one has the best pacing," he said. "The first is where you fall in love with the character but the second is where the writing actually settles into itself." She looked at him more directly now, the mild irritation from a moment ago set aside. "Most people say the first one."

"Most people are wrong."

Something shifted in her expression — not quite a smile, but the shadow of one reconsidered. "He started with animation," she said, the way people volunteer information when they've decided someone might be worth talking to. "I found him through a YouTube video in class eight. One of the early ones, before the studio."

"Class nine for me," he said. "I went through the entire channel in one night." "Same," she said, with a slight widening of the eyes that meant she understood exactly what that night had been like.

They were properly talking now, though neither of them had made a conscious decision to be. They talked about the YouTube channel — the older videos versus the newer ones, the point at which the animation quality had jumped noticeably, the transition into comics and then books and whether the writing had always been there or whether it had developed alongside everything else. She had thoughts about this, specific ones, the kind that come from paying attention rather than just consuming. He found himself actually listening rather than waiting to speak, which was not always his default.

By the time the small crowd had shuffled forward enough to bring them both near the front, they had covered a reasonable portion of Antik's catalogue and arrived at one respectful disagreement about the thriller novel — she thought the ending earned its ambiguity, he thought it withheld a little too much — which neither of them resolved.

Then it was their turn.

Antik Mahmud looked up, smiled the easy smile of someone who genuinely liked meeting the people who followed his work, and asked their names. He signed Nafis' new book with a quick note in the margin. He signed the girl's copy of the second Noman volume — paused when he saw how worn it was, registered this with visible appreciation — and wrote something slightly longer inside the cover that she didn't show Nafis and he didn't ask about.

The selfie was slightly awkward the way selfies with three people always are, Nafis' arm slightly too long, Antik laughing at something at the exact moment the shutter went off. They both checked their phones afterward with the quiet satisfaction of people who had gotten what they came for and knew it. They stepped back from the stall together and stood at the edge of the now-dispersing group, signed copies in hand, the particular pleasant daze of the moment settling over both of them.

"He noticed the wear on the spine," she said, almost to herself. "He actually looked at it."

"He's like that," Nafis said. "There's a video — not a recent one, one of the older behind-the-scenes things — where he talks about how much the early famtiks meant to him. The ones who were there before it got big."

She turned to look at him. "I never saw that one."

"It's buried. 2018 maybe, 2019. Easy to miss if you didn't go back through everything."

She was quiet for a moment, looking down at the worn comic in her hands. "I went back through everything," she said.

"So did I," he said.

Another beat. Around them the Mela moved in its enormous, unhurried way — thousands of people and their afternoon and their books.

"Amaya," she said.

"Nafis."

She nodded once. Then she looked at her signed copy, tucked it carefully against the worn one, and turned to go. Paused.

"That video," she said. "Do you remember what it was titled?"

He thought for a second. "Something about the studio, I think. Early days, before the Silver Play Button. I can find it."

She looked at him for a moment with the expression of someone filing something away. "I'll find it," she said. And then she walked off into the crowd, unhurried, already looking down at the inscription Antik had written inside her copy.

Nafis stood where she had left him, new book in hand, and watched the space where she had been for a moment longer than was strictly necessary.

He did not know her last name or her college or what she did when she wasn't at book fairs. He knew she had been a famtik since class eight, that she had an opinion about narrative ambiguity, and that she had brought a worn copy of a comic to get signed rather than buying a new one — which told him, in the specific language of people who actually care about things, something small but distinct. He told himself it was a chance encounter. February, the Boi Mela, famtiks finding each other the way they sometimes did. These things happened.

He walked back into the crowd, and somewhere ahead of him in the same crowd, she was reading what Antik had written inside her copy and thinking, only briefly, about a video she intended to find herself.
